04 — Color Theory

Why These Pairings

Complementary — Verdigris

↔

Oxblood sits at 359° on the wheel; its true complement lands near 179° — a weathered teal, the color of oxidized copper roofing common across Northeast architecture. Use sparingly (5–10%) as the one cool note against an otherwise warm palette: a link, a chart line, a single accent shape.

Analogous — Brass

↔ ↔

Neighbors on the wheel (30° either side) stay harmonious because they share the same underlying warmth. Brass (29°) is the one worth keeping — it reads as hardware, rivets, cast fittings. The plum neighbor (329°) is shown for reference but isn't part of the working palette; two warm reds this close together compete rather than complement.

07 — Application

Do / Don't

Do

Let Oxblood carry the wordmark and one hero moment per page. Surround it with Linen or Warm White so it reads as a considered accent, not a wash of color.

Don't

Fill entire backgrounds with full-strength Oxblood behind body copy — at that scale it turns heavy and hard to read. Use the 700/900 shades for large dark fields instead.

Do

Use Verdigris only for interactive or informational elements — links, active states, a single chart series. It should feel like a discovery, not a co-equal brand color.

Don't

Pair Oxblood directly with the 329° plum neighbor shown in section 04. Two warm reds of similar intensity fight for attention instead of settling into a hierarchy.
